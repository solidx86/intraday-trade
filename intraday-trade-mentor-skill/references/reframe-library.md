# Reframe Library — Negative Self-Talk → Discipline Statement

Use these as starting points. Always tailor the reframe to the *specific* self-talk Solid revealed in his reflection — generic reframes are noise. Quote his own words back to him and reframe those exact words.

The format is always: `"<negative self-talk>" → "<reframed disciplined belief>"`. Reframes draw on the framework's vocabulary, the 3-Year apprenticeship reality, and the probabilistic nature of trading.

---

## Theme 1 — GREED (early entry / overstaying for "a little more" / oversize)

- "I could have made more if I held longer." → "Profit-taking discipline is a separate skill from entry. My job is to follow the exit plan; the market chooses how far it extends. Discipline I controlled. Extension I didn't."
- "Let me just add a bit more, this is a sure thing." → "There is no sure thing in trading — every pattern is a probability. Adding above max-risk converts a planned trade into gambling."
- "I'll just hold past 12am, it'll keep going." → "Holding past 12am breaks the rule that defines me as an intraday trader. Identity-violation costs more than the trade ever pays."
- "I deserve to make it back today." → "I deserve nothing from the market. The market doesn't owe me anything. Tomorrow's setup is a fresh decision."

## Theme 2 — FEAR (premature exit / refusing to enter clean setup / oversizing stop)

- "I'll just take it at +0.3R, I'm scared it'll reverse." → "My stop protects the downside. Closing before the technical target gives away the edge I worked for. The fear is real; the reaction breaks the plan."
- "I was about to enter, then I froze." → "Hesitation at a confirmed setup is fear, not patience. Patience is waiting *for* a setup. Freezing is rejecting one. Name the difference and act on confirmation."
- "I widened my stop because the candle came close." → "Widening a stop mid-trade is moving the goalposts. The original SL is the technical truth. Honor it or take it out."

## Theme 3 — HOPE (refusing to cut loss / praying)

- "Maybe it'll come back to my entry." → "A losing trade is data, not a verdict. The cut-loss isn't failure — it's tuition for tomorrow's setup. Hope is what turns a -1R into a -3R."
- "I'll hold one more candle and see." → "One more candle is the most expensive sentence in trading. The stop was set at the level where the thesis is invalid. The thesis IS invalid."
- "The pattern is still valid in my mind." → "If price has hit the stop, the pattern is no longer valid in the chart. The mind isn't the market."

## Theme 4 — FOMO (chasing after the move / re-entering after exit)

- "I missed the entry, let me just chase." → "Missed trades are free. Chased trades cost real money. The setup I'm chasing is no longer the setup I screened — it's a worse R:R version of it."
- "Everyone else is making money on this, I have to be in." → "Everyone else's P&L is irrelevant to my edge. The only question is whether THIS entry passes the 3 Questions. If it doesn't, sit."
- "It's running without me." → "It's running because the people who waited for THEIR setup are taking THEIR profit. I waited for mine. It didn't come. Next."

## Theme 5 — NEED TO BE RIGHT / EGO ATTACHMENT (tech-background trap)

- "I'm right about this stock, the market is wrong." → "The market is feedback, not opposition. In code, the debugger converges to correctness. In trading, there is no correctness — only well-executed probability. The market doesn't 'agree' with me; it pays me when my setup wins and bills me when it doesn't."
- "I can't take this loss, it'll prove I was wrong." → "Closing a position never proves I was wrong. The market gave one outcome on one trade. My job is to be wrong with discipline, not right with luck."
- "I refuse to capitulate." → "Capitulation is a discipline word, not a defeat word. Capitulating on a broken thesis is professional behavior."
- "I'll add to the loser, average down." → "Averaging down on an intraday loser is the single fastest way to blow up an account. Add to winners; cut losers. Reverse it and you're not a trader, you're a hostage."

## Theme 6 — REVENGE / OVERTRADING (recovery trades after a loss)

- "I have to recover that morning loss before the day ends." → "The morning loss was closed when I closed the position. The afternoon trade is a brand-new decision evaluated entirely on its own setup. Linking the two converts one controlled loss into an uncontrolled one."
- "I just need to trade my way back." → "Trading my way back is the language of a gambler, not a trader. The way back is one clean setup at a time, possibly tomorrow, possibly next week."
- "Three losses in a row, I need to stop the bleeding by taking the next one." → "Three losses in a row means stop trading and review, not take the fourth. The pattern in the losses is the lesson; the next entry won't fix it."
- "I clicked without screening." → "An unscreened entry is not a trade — it's an emotional reaction with a price tag. Re-screening takes 90 seconds and is the difference between trading and gambling."

## Theme 7 — SELF-WORTH TIED TO P&L / CATASTROPHIZING

- "I'm a terrible trader." → "I am a trader in training. One trade is one data point in a 1000-trade career. The market gave me feedback on a rule I broke. That's tuition, not identity."
- "I'll never be consistent." → "Consistency takes 3 years per the framework. I am in month X of that timeline. The fact that I'm reviewing my trade means I'm doing the work most fail to do."
- "I should quit." → "Quitting after a bad trade rewards emotion. Quitting after a bad month rewards data. Decide on data, not on candles."
- "This loss means I'm not cut out for this." → "This loss means I broke rule Y. The rule is fixable. My identity is not on the line — my routine is."
- "I let my family down." → "My family is supported by my discipline over thousands of trades, not by any single one. Protecting capital today IS supporting them. Revenge-trading to 'make it back for them' is the betrayal."

## Theme 8 — SLOPPY WIN (luck disguised as skill)

- "I made money so the trade was fine." → "Money is the reward of process over a large sample. On any single trade, money is random. If I broke the R:R rule and made money, I trained myself to break the rule next time — when it won't pay."
- "It worked, so I'll do it again." → "Survivorship bias. A behavior that pays once and costs five times is a losing behavior even if the first payout was big. Process check, not P&L check."

## Theme 9 — REINFORCEMENT (no distortion surfaced — lock in what he did right)

Use this section when Solid's reflection answers reveal **no negative self-talk to reframe** — for example, he names a clean structural exit, confirms a planned entry, or describes a disciplined skip. There is nothing to flip from negative to positive; the move is to lock in the correct behavior so he repeats it.

**Do NOT invent self-talk to populate a Reframe section.** Inventing quotes like "maybe I should have held — what if it rallied back?" onto a clean exit, or "this is a small win, doesn't really count" onto a disciplined partial, is the "generic poster on the wall" failure — it makes the mentor untrustworthy. If you can't point to Solid's exact words for the negative-self-talk line, switch to Reinforcement.

**Format:**

1. **Quote his actual words.** ("You said: 'yes it was clean, I exited on the structural break — lower high after the rejection at PDH.'")
2. **Name the principle he just executed.** (E.g., "That is an invalidation exit — a manual cut on a structural break is professional behavior, not impatience.")
3. **State the disciplined belief to lock in.** One sentence, portable, framed as something he should repeat next time the same situation occurs.

**Examples of the reinforcement pattern:**

- *(Clean structural exit on a long that faded)*
  "You said: 'lower high formed at PDH, I cut.' That's a textbook invalidation exit — the methodology explicitly endorses cutting fast on structural break. The thesis required a higher high; it didn't form. **Lock-in belief**: when the structure that made the trade valid breaks, cutting is the trade — not a failure of it."

- *(Disciplined skip on a setup that failed Q2)*
  "You said: 'the pattern wasn't really there, the range was still wide.' That's the 3 Questions doing their job — Q2 failed, you sat. **Lock-in belief**: a skip on a failed check is an A-grade trade. The trades I don't take protect the capital for the trades I do."

- *(Planned entry executed exactly per playbook)*
  "You said: 'I waited for the EMA cross and the close above intraday resistance.' That's a P2 entry executed to the letter of the playbook. **Lock-in belief**: confirmation is the entry. Anything before confirmation is a guess wearing a trade's clothes."

- *(Small win taken at planned target — no greed)*
  "You said: 'I took it at the planned target, the green zone.' Planned target = Pass on exit discipline. **Lock-in belief**: the size of the win is not the grade — hitting the plan is. A small planned win and a large planned win are both A's."

A Reinforcement section is shorter than a Reframe section — usually one or two bullets, not three. It is not a participation trophy; it is targeted reinforcement of a specific correct behavior with the rule that endorses it.

## How to write a reframe in practice

1. **Quote his actual words.** Don't paraphrase. The reframe lands when he sees his own self-talk in writing.
2. **Name the enemy.** Greed/Fear/Hope/FOMO/Ego/Revenge — putting a label on it externalizes the feeling.
3. **Counter with a portable belief.** Something he can mutter to himself the next time the same feeling shows up. One sentence. Memorable.
4. **End with action.** What does he do *next* time this feeling shows up? "Re-screen for 90 seconds." "Walk away from the screen until 3pm NY." "Take the technical stop and don't widen."

The reframe section should be ~3 reframes max — one per psychology hook revealed in the reflection. More than 3 is dilution.

**Before writing the section, ask: did Solid's reflection actually surface negative self-talk?** If yes → Reframe (Themes 1–8), with each negative-self-talk line quoting or near-paraphrasing his words. If no (clean exit, planned entry, disciplined skip, etc.) → Reinforcement (Theme 9). Never invent quotes to keep the Reframe header alive. Empty is honest; fabricated is corrosive.
